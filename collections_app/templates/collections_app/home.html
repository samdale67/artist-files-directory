Why An Artist Files Directory?

If you are new to artist files, head to What Are Artist Files? for a primer, or spend some time at artistfiles.arlisna.org, a professional resource covering all aspects of artist files.

Researchers who know about artist files find them indispensable, yet they continue to be among the least accessible resources in art research collections. Researchers rely on artist files to establish chronologies, expand exhibition histories, review stylistic developments, and assess the critical reception of artists over time, among many other uses. They provide documentation about well established artists, and even more importantly, capture material on regional and lesser-known artists. Curators value them for special objects such as those highlighted in the The New York Public Library's collection, which add dimension to exhibitions.

❝ ... artist files continue to be among the least accessible resources in art research collections ...  ❞

The very nature of the material (mainly their typically small size, making itemized cataloging prohibitive for most collectors), lack of representation in union catalogs such as WorldCat, and limited staff resources to manage them in general are chief causes of their low exposure. They are also among the last collections to be digitized due to their physical variety and sheer volume, not to mention copyright challenges.

This directory aims to help researchers discover these collections. It also helps managers connect with colleagues to learn about different approaches to collecting, provisioning, and preserving these collections. It is also a tool for helping connect institutional collections with private collections. Likewise, the directory helps files with subject concentrations connect with each other.

What is the Scope of the Directory?

While the first Artist Files Revealed publication (Art Libraries Society of North America, 2009, 2010) and related (now defunct) online directory focused on institutional artist files collections, the new directory expands coverage by offering access to personal collections. The directory will help reveal specialized sets of artist research material collected by curators, professors, scholars, and private collectors and facilitate connections with institutional collectors. While the directory focuses on printed artist files material, collectors can describe digital content and projects.

A number of new access options help researchers find collections by subject, technical data such as special formats, physical location of the the collection, and even by collector categories. Collection images, also new, allow collectors to highlight special items or storage systems. It also provides a dealer directory for sellers who specialize in artist ephemera. Quick searches pull together consortial/shared, digital, and new collections. An RSS feed notifies users when a new collection gets added to the directory. And, just for fun, random collection provides a roulette wheel experience.

A major caveat: the directory is not a delivery platform for digitized or born-digital artist files content. While the the directory allows collectors to describe digital material and projects, this platform is not designed to be a storage or access system for these collections. Instead, it points researchers to these collections.
